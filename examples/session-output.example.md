**Prompt:**

```
/ask-the-board should I take the ₱30k/month agency retainer or keep freelancing?
```

**Output:**

## 🏛️ Board Session: Take the ₱30k retainer, or stay fully freelance?

### 🎨 Lena Cruz says
Ate, look at your own numbers — ₱18k to ₱45k a month is a rollercoaster, and 40% of your hours are unpaid revisions and invoice-chasing. That instability is what keeps you saying yes to bad-fit clients. A retainer is *boring money*, and boring money pays for brave work.

- Take it as a **cash floor**, but cap it at **60% of your hours** — you're renting out prime capacity, not selling all of it.
- Use the stable base to finally introduce your **two-rounds-then-a-rate revision tax** on every *other* client.
- Put a deposit + contract on everything else. "Contracts are kindness" — it protects the relationship, not just the peso.

### ⚡ Marcus Reyes says
Careful. ₱30k for your best hours might be a discount you'll regret. You don't have a demand problem, Maya — you have a *price* problem. You undercharge because you think "no credentials" means "no leverage." It doesn't. Proof beats credentials.

- Before you sign anything, **raise your project minimum toward ₱25k now** — a rate increase is pure margin; a retainer is capped upside.
- If you take it, treat it as a **floor you outgrow**, not a home. Renegotiate or exit at the first review.
- On the portfolio: don't be "a designer." Be **the F&B launch designer**. Category of one sets its own price.

### ✅ Where they agree
- **Your pricing, not your workload, is the real problem.** Both want the number fixed first.
- **Stop giving away revisions and free scope.** Lena's revision tax and Marcus's "say-no muscle" are the same fix.
- **The portfolio should sell outcomes, not pretty pictures** — packages and named results (Feb 28 launch).

### ⚔️ Where they clash
Lena optimizes for **stability** — lock in a cash floor so fear stops driving your decisions. Marcus optimizes for **upside** — a retainer can quietly cap you right when you should be raising rates. This isn't one of them being wrong; it's different risk appetites. Lena is treating your *perfectionism spiral and quiet-when-embarrassed* pattern as the top risk (income shocks feed it). Marcus is treating *chronic undercharging* as the top risk.

### 🎯 Coach's synthesis — what you should do
Your profile says deadlines motivate you and you cave on price when you can't justify the number — so let's justify it and put dates on it. Take the retainer, but **as Lena's capped floor, with Marcus's raise built in from day one.** The floor removes the fear that makes you undercharge; you spend that safety raising everything else. You don't have to choose stability *or* upside — you sequence them.

1. **By Jan 31** — Accept the retainer, in writing, **capped at 60% of your weekly hours** (≈24 hrs), with a review date set for April. That's your deadline anyway.
2. **By Feb 7** — Raise your project minimum to **₱25k** and add the two-rounds revision rule to your standard contract. Send it to your next new lead — no exceptions.
3. **By Feb 28** — Launch the portfolio as an **F&B-launch specialist**: 3 case studies led by results, priced packages, deposit-required. Not a gallery.

💸 Money check: your ~₱6k/month stress food-delivery leak is roughly one week of the retainer's stability buying you nothing. When the retainer lands, automate ₱6k/month into savings the same day it arrives — before it becomes DoorDash.
